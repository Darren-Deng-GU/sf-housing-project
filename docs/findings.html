Findings & Visualizations

Each visualization below is fully interactive—hover for details, zoom with scroll, and use the toolbar to export as PNG. All charts are generated from data processed through our Snowflake pipeline.

1. Residential Housing Types

Single-family homes dominate San Francisco's residential landscape, representing the largest share of parcels. This concentration of single-family zoning is a central factor in the housing shortage—it limits density in neighborhoods where multi-family housing could significantly increase supply.

2. Building Permits Issued Over Time

Permit issuance shows clear cyclical patterns tied to economic conditions. Over-the-Counter (OTC) Alterations consistently dominate, peaking above 37,000 annually in 2019. A sharp decline is visible during the 2008 financial crisis, followed by a gradual recovery. The COVID-19 pandemic triggered another steep drop in 2020—OTC permits fell from ~37,000 to ~19,500—with only partial recovery since. New construction wood-frame permits declined from ~375/year (2000) to under 50 by 2025, signaling a structural slowdown in new housing supply.

3. Changes in Building Use

The Sankey diagram reveals the most common transitions between building use categories. The largest flow is Single-Family to Two-Family conversions (3,111 permits), followed by Two-Family to Apartments (1,816) and Retail to Food/Beverage (1,723). These patterns reflect two trends: densification of residential neighborhoods through unit subdivision, and commercial adaptation as retail spaces convert to restaurants and food services—a shift accelerated by changing consumer habits and remote work.

4. Permit Processing Time Distribution

Processing times vary dramatically by permit type. New Construction permits show the widest distribution, with some projects taking over 1,000 days from filing to completion. Even common Additions/Alterations permits show significant spread, with medians often exceeding 100 days. These delays represent a direct drag on housing supply—every month of bureaucratic delay is a month that new units are not available to residents, compounding the affordability crisis.

5. Top Neighborhoods by Permit Activity

Development activity is heavily concentrated in a handful of neighborhoods. Financial District/South Beach leads with over 135,000 permits, followed by Sunset/Parkside (~79,000) and the Mission District (~78,000). The top five neighborhoods account for roughly one-third of all permit activity citywide, suggesting that development benefits—and pressures—are unevenly distributed across San Francisco.

6. House Price Index (1975–2024)

The All-Transactions House Price Index for the San Francisco MSA shows a long-term upward trajectory with notable disruptions—the dot-com bust (2000), the financial crisis (2008), and the pandemic dip (2020). Despite periodic corrections, prices have consistently recovered to new highs, underscoring the structural nature of the Bay Area's affordability challenge.

7. Median Rent Across the Bay Area

This interactive map visualizes median gross rent across the nine Bay Area counties using 2023 American Community Survey data. San Mateo County has the highest median rent ($3,006), followed by Santa Clara ($2,934) and Marin ($2,603). San Francisco itself sits at $2,583. The most affordable counties are Sonoma ($2,072) and Solano ($2,143)—yet even these "affordable" areas far exceed national averages. Click on county markers for detailed statistics including median income and population.

8. Permit Volume vs. House Price Index

Cross-analysis: This dual-axis chart overlays annual permit volume (bars) with the house price index (line). A striking pattern emerges: while house prices climbed steadily from an index of ~170 (2000) to over 500 (2025), permit volume peaked around 2018–2019 and then sharply declined post-COVID. The divergence since 2020—rising prices amid falling permits—suggests that reduced construction activity is further tightening supply and accelerating price increases, creating a feedback loop that deepens the affordability crisis.

Key Takeaways

Permit Bottleneck

Processing times frequently exceed 100 days and can stretch beyond 1,000 days for complex projects, representing a significant barrier to new housing supply. Streamlining the approval process could accelerate unit delivery.

Concentrated Development

The top 5 neighborhoods account for roughly one-third of all permits, with Financial District/South Beach alone contributing over 135,000. Development benefits are not evenly distributed across the city.

Use Conversion Trend

Commercial-to-residential conversions are increasing, presenting a policy opportunity to incentivize adaptive reuse of underutilized commercial space.

Future Directions

This proof-of-concept demonstrates the viability of a scalable analytics pipeline for housing policy research. With additional funding, we propose expanding in three directions:

Scale to 50 States

Apply the same pipeline to permit data from all major U.S. metro areas, enabling cross-city comparisons of regulatory efficiency.

Predictive Modeling

Build ML models to forecast permit processing times based on project characteristics, neighborhood, and historical patterns.

Real-Time Dashboard

Deploy a live dashboard with automated data refresh, enabling policymakers to monitor development trends as they unfold.
