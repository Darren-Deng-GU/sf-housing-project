Findings & Visualizations

Every visualization below is fully interactive. Hover for details, zoom with the scroll wheel, and use the toolbar to export as PNG. All charts come from data processed through our Snowflake pipeline.

1. Residential Housing Types

Single-family homes dominate San Francisco's residential landscape and make up the largest share of parcels. This concentration of single-family zoning is a central driver of the housing shortage. It limits density in neighborhoods where multi-family housing could significantly expand supply.

2. Building Permits Issued Over Time

Permit issuance tracks economic cycles. Over-the-Counter (OTC) Alterations consistently dominate, peaking above 37,000 annually in 2019. Issuance dropped sharply during the 2008 financial crisis and recovered gradually. The COVID-19 pandemic triggered another steep decline in 2020, with OTC permits falling from about 37,000 to 19,500. Only a partial recovery has followed. New construction wood-frame permits dropped from about 375 per year in 2000 to under 50 by 2025, signaling a structural slowdown in new housing supply.

3. Changes in Building Use

The Sankey diagram shows the most common transitions between building use categories. The largest flow is Single-Family to Two-Family conversions (3,111 permits), followed by Two-Family to Apartments (1,816) and Retail to Food/Beverage (1,723). Two trends emerge here: densification of residential neighborhoods through unit subdivision, and commercial adaptation as retail space converts to restaurants and food services. Changing consumer habits and remote work have accelerated the commercial shift.

4. Permit Processing Time Distribution

Processing times vary dramatically by permit type. New Construction permits show the widest distribution, with some projects taking over 1,000 days from filing to completion. Even common Additions and Alterations permits show significant spread, with medians often above 100 days. These delays drag directly on housing supply. Every month of bureaucratic delay keeps new units off the market and compounds the affordability crisis.

5. Top Neighborhoods by Permit Activity

Development activity concentrates in a handful of neighborhoods. Financial District/South Beach leads with over 135,000 permits, followed by Sunset/Parkside (about 79,000) and the Mission District (about 78,000). The top five neighborhoods account for roughly one-third of all permit activity citywide. Both the benefits and pressures of development are distributed unevenly across San Francisco.

6. House Price Index (1975 to 2024)

The All-Transactions House Price Index for the San Francisco MSA trends steadily upward, with notable disruptions during the dot-com bust (2000), the financial crisis (2008), and the pandemic dip (2020). Prices have recovered to new highs after every correction. That pattern underscores the structural nature of the Bay Area's affordability challenge.

7. Median Rent Across the Bay Area

This interactive map shows median gross rent across the nine Bay Area counties, using 2023 American Community Survey data. San Mateo County has the highest median rent at $3,006, followed by Santa Clara ($2,934) and Marin ($2,603). San Francisco sits at $2,583. Sonoma ($2,072) and Solano ($2,143) are the most affordable counties, yet both still exceed national averages by a wide margin. Click any county marker for details on median income and population.

8. Permit Volume vs. House Price Index

Cross-analysis: This dual-axis chart overlays annual permit volume (bars) with the house price index (line). House prices climbed steadily from an index of about 170 in 2000 to over 500 by 2025. Permit volume peaked around 2018 to 2019 and dropped sharply after COVID. Since 2020, rising prices have coincided with falling permits. Reduced construction appears to be tightening supply and accelerating price increases, creating a feedback loop that deepens the affordability crisis.

Key Takeaways

Permit Bottleneck

Processing times frequently exceed 100 days and can stretch past 1,000 days for complex projects. Streamlining the approval process could accelerate unit delivery.

Concentrated Development

The top 5 neighborhoods account for roughly one-third of all permits. Financial District/South Beach alone contributes over 135,000. Development benefits are distributed unevenly across the city.

Use Conversion Trend

Commercial-to-residential conversions are rising. That creates a policy opportunity to incentivize adaptive reuse of underutilized commercial space.

Future Directions

This proof-of-concept shows the viability of a scalable analytics pipeline for housing policy research. With additional funding, we propose expanding in three directions:

Scale to 50 States

Apply the same pipeline to permit data from all major U.S. metro areas and enable cross-city comparisons of regulatory efficiency.

Predictive Modeling

Build ML models to forecast permit processing times from project characteristics, neighborhood, and historical patterns.

Real-Time Dashboard

Deploy a live dashboard with automated data refresh so policymakers can monitor development trends as they unfold.
